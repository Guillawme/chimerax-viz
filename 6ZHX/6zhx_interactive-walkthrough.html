How to read this?

This page is an interactive document meant to be read from the help browser of UCSF ChimeraX. Links displayed in this style are regular links that your regular web browser can follow, while links displayed like this are special: your normal web browser will not do anything with them, but clicking them while you are reading the page from the help browser in ChimeraX will run commands to control the ChimeraX session.

To read this page in ChimeraX, open it and run the following command at the prompt (replace https://url/to/this/file.html by the address you are seeing in your regular web browser while reading this):

open https://url/to/this/file.html

Interaction between the linker of ALC1 and the nucleosome acidic patch

This interactive document will guide you through the structure reported in the following publication:

Lehmann LC, Bacic L, Hewitt G, Brackmann K, Sabantsev A, Gaullier G, Pytharopoulou S, Degliesposti G, Okkenhaug H, Tan S, Costa A, Skehel JM, Boulton SJ & Deindl S (2020) Mechanistic Insights into Regulation of the ALC1 Remodeler by the Nucleosome Acidic Patch. Cell Reports 33 doi:10.1016/j.celrep.2020.108529

Preparing the session

We will download PDB entry 6ZHX and EMDB entry EMD-11220, which combined weigh a total of about 110 MB, so this will take a few seconds to complete, depending on your internet connection speed (next time you run this document it will be almost instant, because ChimeraX keeps a local copy of downloaded files). In addition, we will apply the same representations and color code as in the article figure. When you are ready, prepare the session.

At this stage, you should see the cryo-EM map at an appropriate contour level. Feel free to explore the map interactively before we continue. If you get lost, you can:

  • reset the orientation,
  • reset the contour level,
  • or reset both.

Looking at the cryo-EM map

If you are not familiar with the nucleosome, some color code will help: apply color code. At this stage, and assuming your went back to the initial orientation (or never moved away from it), you should see the cryo-EM map colored by chain assignment just like in Figure 2E of the article. The feature of this map discussed in the article is the presence of a peptide from ALC1 (pink) bound to the acidic patch formed by histones H2A (yellow) and H2B (red). H3 is shown in blue, H4 in green and the DNA in grey.

In the article, we only showed one side of the nucleosome because of space limitations. But the nucleosome is mostly symmetric, and therefore has a second H2A-H2B dimer and its acidic patch on the other side. If you look at the other side, you will see that there is an ALC1 peptide bound there as well (cyan). You can click on this last link as many times as you want, to rotate the nucleosome 180 degrees around the vertical axis.

Intepreting the map with an atomic model

Looking at the map is good, but will only take us so far. We need to interpret it in terms of atoms. The map looks very strong for the nucleosome (side chain density is clearly visible), slightly less so for the bound ALC1 peptide. We can first look at an atomic model with the histones represented as their molecular surface and the ALC1 peptide as spheres. Display atomic model.

Let's zoom in on the peptide. Let's also change its display style to sticks, to better see the atoms.

Feel free to rotate the view to look at it from any side. If you get lost, you can recenter the view on the peptide.

We can now show the density map around the peptide to check that the model is supported by the data. Again, here feel free to rotate the view or zoom in or out. The reset link above will bring you back to the view centered on the peptide, should you need it.

Finally, let's have a look at the hydrogen bond network between the peptide and the histones.

Comparison with the LANA peptide

At this point, we have interactively walked through Figures 2E and 2F of the article. To visit Figure 2G, we need to download PDB entry 1ZLA which contains the LANA peptide bound to the acidic patch. We also need to align it to our nucleosome model and adjust a few visual parameters. Finally, let's zoom in on LANA.

Again, feel free to look around. Recenter on LANA if you get lost.

Almost there! We can finally also display the interactions between LANA and the nucleosome, and you now have a view similar to Figure 2G of the article.
